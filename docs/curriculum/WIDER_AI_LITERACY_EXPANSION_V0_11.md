# Wider AI Literacy Expansion v0.11

Date: 2026-06-04

Purpose: plan a new Wider AI Literacy section that extends Prompt Life beyond model mechanics into origins, benefits, costs, responsibility, and human-centered use. This is a planning document only; no Journey cards are implemented in app data in this pass.

Audience: smart high-schoolers, first-year college students, faculty, higher-ed IT leaders, and PhDs outside computer science.

Tone: serious, humane, precise, not utopian, not alarmist.

## Framing

Generative AI did not appear from nowhere. It emerged from a convergence of human-created digital data, compute and storage infrastructure, deep-learning research, economic incentives, human labeling and evaluation labor, and internet-scale connectivity.

The new Wider AI Literacy section should help learners ask:

- Who created the data?
- Who gave consent?
- Who is compensated?
- Who pays energy and water costs?
- Who benefits?
- Who loses skills, jobs, privacy, or agency?
- How do humans remain centered?

## Recommended Placement

The current app has 26 Journey cards. The existing Wider AI Literacy area contains `How AI Learns`, `Diffusion vs Autoregression`, `Multimodal AI`, and `Risk vs Myth`. The v0.11 expansion should eventually replace the single broad `How AI Learns` umbrella with a more intentional sequence after `RAG and Retrieval`, `Grounding`, and `Hallucinations`, then lead into `Risk vs Myth` and the final synthesis.

## Card 1: The Perfect Storm

- Title: The Perfect Storm
- Subtitle: Why LLMs arrived now
- Path label: Essential Path
- One-sentence definition: Modern LLMs became possible when human-created digital data, powerful compute and storage, deep-learning methods, human feedback labor, and economic incentives converged.
- Core explanation: LLMs did not arrive because of one magical breakthrough. They came from a storm front where internet-scale data, GPUs and accelerators, cloud storage, deep-learning algorithms, transformer architecture, human annotation and feedback labor, and market incentives collided. The transformer mattered, but it worked inside a larger ecosystem of data, hardware, networks, research, and capital.
- Where it fits in the Journey: Wider AI Literacy, after learners understand training, inference, context, RAG, and generation mechanics. It widens the question from "How does a prompt become a response?" to "Why did this kind of model become possible now?"
- Why it matters: This lowers mystery and hype at once. Learners see LLMs as the result of many technical and social conditions, not as a sudden magic object.
- How it connects to previous/next concepts: Previous: mechanics showed what the model does during training and inference. Next: `Collective Intelligence, Extracted` asks whose data and labor made the storm possible.
- Metaphor: A storm front where data, compute, algorithms, human labor, and incentives collide.
- Brain/cognition metaphor: Like a mind shaped by exposure, practice, and feedback.
- Where the brain metaphor breaks: The model has no lived experience. It absorbs statistical structure from human-made data rather than living a human life.
- Misconception addressed: LLMs appeared because of one magical breakthrough.
- Suggested visual aid: Four rivers flowing into a glowing origami transformer: Data, Compute, Algorithms, Human labor. Economic incentives appear as wind/current shaping the rivers.
- Suggested exercise/checkpoint: Sort "storm ingredient" cards into Data, Compute, Algorithms, Human labor, and Incentives. Checkpoint: "Which answer best explains why LLMs arrived now?"
- Glossary terms: training data, compute, accelerator, GPU, transformer, human feedback learning, annotation, cloud infrastructure, economic incentive
- Source needs: SOURCE NEEDED: transformer architecture; scaling and compute infrastructure; cloud storage/data-center history; human annotation and feedback labor; market/investment context.

## Card 2: Collective Intelligence, Extracted

- Title: Collective Intelligence, Extracted
- Subtitle: No creators, no model
- Path label: Ethics Path
- One-sentence definition: Generative AI depends on patterns learned from human-created language, art, code, documents, and culture.
- Core explanation: Useful GenAI depends on human-produced data: books, websites, code repositories, forums, art, documentation, journalism, research, and conversation. Much of this collection happened without meaningful consent, transparency, or compensation. The model is not humanity's mind; it is a system trained on traces of human expression, filtered through datasets, optimization, and product choices.
- Where it fits in the Journey: Wider AI Literacy, immediately after `The Perfect Storm`. It turns the "data river" into a moral question.
- Why it matters: Learners need to understand that model capability is built from human culture and labor. That makes provenance, consent, compensation, and accountability central rather than decorative.
- How it connects to previous/next concepts: Previous: the storm made LLMs possible. Next: `Benefits Worth Taking Seriously` asks what this extracted capability can responsibly help humans do.
- Metaphor: A lantern lit by millions of borrowed flames.
- Brain/cognition metaphor: Like a person shaped by culture, teachers, books, and conversation.
- Where the brain metaphor breaks: A person participates consciously in culture. The model statistically absorbs patterns without consent, gratitude, responsibility, or understanding.
- Misconception addressed: The model created its abilities alone.
- Suggested visual aid: A paper lantern made of many human-created pages, glowing into a model cloud. Keep all labels in HTML, not in the image.
- Suggested exercise/checkpoint: "Created, Collected, or Generated?" Sort items such as published article, open-source code, scraped forum post, model answer, generated image, and licensed dataset.
- Glossary terms: training data, data provenance, consent, copyright, compensation, collective intelligence, extraction, dataset
- Source needs: SOURCE NEEDED: data provenance; copyright and compensation disputes; dataset transparency; creator consent; journalism/art/code collection practices.

## Card 3: Benefits Worth Taking Seriously

- Title: Benefits Worth Taking Seriously
- Subtitle: Real help, not utopian hype
- Path label: Essential Path
- One-sentence definition: AI benefits are strongest when models amplify human judgment, accessibility, creativity, research, and learning without replacing accountability.
- Core explanation: Model literacy should not dismiss benefits. AI can help with accessibility, translation, summarization, writing assistance, coding assistance, document search and RAG, supervised tutoring support, research triage, administrative drafting, creative brainstorming, and scientific workflow support. These benefits should be separated into evidence tiers: demonstrated, plausible, and speculative. Billionaire utopia claims should not be stated as fact.
- Where it fits in the Journey: Wider AI Literacy, after the extraction card and before the costs card. The learner should see that ethical critique is not anti-benefit.
- Why it matters: A humane curriculum should count real help without sliding into hype. Faculty and IT leaders need this distinction for procurement, policy, and classroom practice.
- How it connects to previous/next concepts: Previous: human-created data powers model capabilities. Next: `Costs We Must Count` asks what must be paid, protected, or redesigned while pursuing benefits.
- Metaphor: A tool garden, not a magic kingdom.
- Brain/cognition metaphor: Like having a tireless assistant that can draft, search, translate, and suggest.
- Where the brain metaphor breaks: The assistant has no judgment, responsibility, or lived understanding.
- Misconception addressed: AI benefits require believing in imminent utopia.
- Suggested visual aid: A human at center with orbiting tools: translate, summarize, search, draft, code, assist. Use orbiting tool shapes with HTML callouts and evidence-tier chips below.
- Suggested exercise/checkpoint: Sort benefit claims into Demonstrated, Plausible, and Speculative. Include "SOURCE NEEDED" prompts for claims that require evidence before publication.
- Glossary terms: accessibility, translation, summarization, RAG, tutoring support, human review, evidence tier, accountability
- Source needs: SOURCE NEEDED: accessibility benefits; translation quality/use cases; summarization limits; coding assistance studies; document search/RAG; tutoring support with supervision; research triage; administrative workload; scientific workflow support.

### Benefit Evidence Tiers

- Demonstrated: accessibility support, translation support, summarization assistance, writing/coding assistance, document search/RAG in constrained corpora, administrative drafting under human review.
- Plausible: supervised tutoring support, research triage, creative brainstorming, scientific workflow acceleration in well-designed settings.
- Speculative: broad utopian claims, inevitable abundance claims, fully autonomous expert replacement, or claims that AI alone will solve education, health, science, or poverty.

## Card 4: Costs We Must Count

- Title: Costs We Must Count
- Subtitle: The answer feels weightless. The infrastructure is not.
- Path label: Ethics Path
- One-sentence definition: Generative AI has physical, social, cultural, and ethical costs that must be counted honestly.
- Core explanation: AI outputs feel instant and weightless, but they rely on material infrastructure and social systems. Costs include energy demand, water demand, carbon emissions, data center siting, chip supply chains, e-waste, data extraction without consent, copyright and compensation disputes, labor disruption, deskilling, surveillance and privacy, bias amplification, information pollution, human de-centering, and concentration of power. Do not publish precise energy or water numbers until sourced and scoped by model, region, cooling method, electricity source, hardware, and workload.
- Where it fits in the Journey: Wider AI Literacy, after benefits. This makes the curriculum honest without making the learner hopeless.
- Why it matters: Model literacy includes stewardship. Institutions need to ask what they are buying, who is affected, and what tradeoffs are hidden by a clean chat box.
- How it connects to previous/next concepts: Previous: benefits can be real. Next: `Human-Centered AI` asks what values should govern these tradeoffs.
- Metaphor: The invisible factory behind the instant answer.
- Brain/cognition metaphor: Human thought has bodily costs too, but AI costs are industrial and infrastructural.
- Where the brain metaphor breaks: Human metabolism and data-center infrastructure are not the same kind of cost.
- Misconception addressed: Digital means weightless or cost-free.
- Suggested visual aid: A glowing phone connected to data center, power grid, cooling water, chip plant, workers, and training data.
- Suggested exercise/checkpoint: "Cost Map" matching exercise: connect AI use to energy, water/cooling, chips, data, labor, privacy, and power concentration.
- Glossary terms: compute, data center, energy demand, water use, carbon emissions, e-waste, labor disruption, deskilling, privacy, bias, information pollution
- Source needs: SOURCE NEEDED: environmental footprint of AI/data centers; energy demand; water use; carbon emissions; data-center siting; chip supply chains; e-waste; data extraction; copyright and compensation disputes; labor disruption; deskilling; surveillance/privacy; bias; information pollution; market concentration.

## Card 5: Human-Centered AI

- Title: Human-Centered AI
- Subtitle: Tools should serve human dignity
- Path label: Ethics Path
- One-sentence definition: AI should be judged by whether it supports human dignity, learning, responsibility, creativity, relationships, and the common good.
- Core explanation: Human judgment remains central. Technology should complement, not replace, human intelligence. Authentic learning and relationships matter. Human dignity should not be subordinated to speed, profit, or automation. A model can produce moral language without moral understanding, so humans, institutions, and communities remain responsible for AI decisions and deployments.
- Where it fits in the Journey: Wider AI Literacy, after costs and before the design-choice card. It translates tradeoffs into moral criteria.
- Why it matters: The app should help learners feel capable of judgment, not merely impressed or afraid. Higher-ed audiences especially need language for dignity, learning, responsibility, and the common good.
- How it connects to previous/next concepts: Previous: costs show why values matter. Next: `Better AI Is a Choice` turns values into technical, policy, and incentive choices.
- Metaphor: Tools should fit human hands, not replace human purpose.
- Brain/cognition metaphor: Wisdom is more than fluency.
- Where the brain metaphor breaks: The model can simulate ethical language without moral understanding.
- Misconception addressed: If AI is powerful, it should decide.
- Suggested visual aid: Human figure in a calm zen garden, with AI tools orbiting as instruments rather than rulers.
- Suggested exercise/checkpoint: Reflection: "Where must a human remain accountable in this workflow?" Sort examples into Human judgment required, AI assist useful, and Do not automate.
- Glossary terms: human-centered AI, dignity, responsibility, accountability, common good, human review, alignment, governance
- Source needs: SOURCE NEEDED: Laudato Si'; Dignitas Infinita; Antiqua et nova; Rome Call for AI Ethics; broader secular human-centered AI frameworks; education ethics; human dignity frameworks.

## Card 6: Better AI Is a Choice

- Title: Better AI Is a Choice
- Subtitle: Design, policy, and incentives matter
- Path label: Deep Path
- One-sentence definition: AI costs are not fixed laws of nature; they are shaped by technical design, deployment choices, governance, and economic incentives.
- Core explanation: Better systems can be designed. Mitigations include smaller task-specific models, efficient inference, model distillation, better hardware utilization, low-carbon electricity, water-aware siting, better cooling, transparent data provenance, licensing and creator compensation, opt-out and consent systems, privacy-preserving deployment, RAG instead of retraining when appropriate, human review for high-stakes use, institutional policy, public-interest models, labor transition planning, and independent evaluation.
- Where it fits in the Journey: Wider AI Literacy, after `Human-Centered AI`. It should feel empowering without pretending every problem is easy.
- Why it matters: Learners should not conclude that harms are inevitable if the technology is useful. Choices by developers, institutions, companies, governments, and users shape outcomes.
- How it connects to previous/next concepts: Previous: human-centered values define what should be protected. Next: `Effective Prompting from Model Literacy` gives an individual-level practice while keeping system-level responsibility visible.
- Metaphor: A forked path: extractive AI or responsible AI.
- Brain/cognition metaphor: Like forming better habits before bad habits harden.
- Where the brain metaphor breaks: The model does not choose its own ethics. Humans, institutions, companies, and governments choose the system design.
- Misconception addressed: The harms are inevitable if the technology is useful.
- Suggested visual aid: Two paths through a neon paper landscape: Extractive path and responsible path.
- Suggested exercise/checkpoint: "Choice Point" matrix: match mitigation strategies to the cost they reduce. Include institutional-level and individual-level choices separately.
- Glossary terms: model distillation, efficient inference, data provenance, consent, licensing, privacy-preserving deployment, RAG, human review, governance, independent evaluation
- Source needs: SOURCE NEEDED: efficient model design; model distillation; data-center sustainability; licensing/consent systems; privacy-preserving AI; RAG versus retraining use cases; institutional AI governance; public-interest AI; labor transition planning; independent evaluation.

## Card 7: Effective Prompting from Model Literacy

- Title: Effective Prompting from Model Literacy
- Subtitle: Prompting steers context, not weights
- Path label: Essential Path
- One-sentence definition: Good prompts work because they shape the current context: task, constraints, examples, data, audience, source requirements, uncertainty, and output format.
- Core explanation: This app is not a prompt-engineering tutorial. This card is an epilogue showing how model literacy improves use. Better prompts usually help because they shape the context window for the current run: clear task, relevant context, constraints, examples, desired output format, source/evidence requirements, uncertainty request, and human review. Prompting is not magic wording and usually does not train the model.
- Where it fits in the Journey: Wider AI Literacy, near the end, after systemic responsibility and before final synthesis. It turns model literacy into practical, bounded use.
- Why it matters: Learners can use AI better when they understand context, response tokens, RAG, uncertainty, and human review.
- How it connects to previous/next concepts: Previous: better AI is shaped by choices. Next: `Model Literate Synthesis` asks learners to explain both mechanics and consequences.
- Metaphor: Setting the stage before the performance.
- Brain/cognition metaphor: Like giving a person a clear assignment with examples and constraints.
- Where the brain metaphor breaks: Prompting usually does not train the model or change weights.
- Misconception addressed: Prompting is magic wording or permanent teaching.
- Suggested visual aid: Prompt components entering a context tray.
- Suggested exercise/checkpoint: Sort prompt components into Task, Context, Constraints, Evidence, Output format, Uncertainty, and Human review. Checkpoint: "What does prompting usually change?"
- Glossary terms: prompt, context window, input context, in-context learning, RAG, grounding, uncertainty, human review
- Source needs: SOURCE NEEDED: provider prompting guidance; education use guidance; human review and source/evidence practices; uncertainty communication.

## Card 8: Model Literate Synthesis

- Title: Model Literate Synthesis
- Subtitle: Understand the machine, then decide how to use it
- Path label: Essential Path
- One-sentence definition: Model literacy means understanding both the mechanics and the human consequences.
- Core explanation: A model-literate learner can explain that training changes weights; inference uses weights; context steers the current run; responses are generated one token at a time; RAG retrieves evidence into context; hallucinations are plausible outputs without grounding; benefits are real but not magical; costs are real but not inevitable; and humans remain responsible.
- Where it fits in the Journey: Final Stop. This should be the capstone card after Wider AI Literacy and before Badge completion.
- Why it matters: The goal is not trivia. It is a durable mental model that supports wise use, institutional judgment, and better questions.
- How it connects to previous/next concepts: Previous: prompting and responsible design show bounded action. Next: Badge/reflection asks the learner to teach the model lifecycle and responsibility frame to someone else.
- Metaphor: A map, compass, and lantern.
- Brain/cognition metaphor: Like wisdom: knowledge plus judgment.
- Where the brain metaphor breaks: A model can produce fluent explanations of ethics without itself being wise.
- Misconception addressed: Mechanics alone are enough, or ethics alone is enough.
- Suggested visual aid: Learner holding a map of the model lifecycle and a human-centered compass.
- Suggested exercise/checkpoint: Final teach-back: explain the model lifecycle and one human responsibility in five sentences. Optional reflection: "What will you now ask before using an AI system?"
- Glossary terms: model literacy, training, inference, context window, RAG, grounding, hallucination, human-centered AI, accountability
- Source needs: SOURCE NEEDED: no new mechanism source beyond prior cards; source final synthesis claims to the reviewed mechanism, governance, environmental, education, and ethics sources above.
